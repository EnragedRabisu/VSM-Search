*Marburg virus*^[1] <#cite_note-KuhnArch-1> is a hemorrhagic fever virus
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemorrhagic_fever_virus> of the
/Filoviridae <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filoviridae>/ family of
viruses and a member of the species /Marburg marburgvirus
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marburg_marburgvirus>/, genus
/Marburgvirus <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marburgvirus>/. Marburg
virus (MARV) causes Marburg virus disease
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marburg_virus_disease> in humans
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human> and nonhuman primates
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primates>, a form of viral hemorrhagic
fever <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viral_hemorrhagic_fever>.^[2]
<#cite_note-Spickler-2> The virus is considered to be extremely
dangerous. The WHO
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization> rates it as a
Risk Group 4 Pathogen (requiring biosafety level 4-equivalent
containment
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosafety_level#Biosafety_level_4>).^[3]
<#cite_note-BMBL5-3> In the United States, the NIH
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health>/National
Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_of_Allergy_and_Infectious_Diseases>
ranks it as a Category A Priority Pathogen^[4]
<#cite_note-PriorityPathogens-4> and the Centers for Disease Control and
Prevention
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention>
lists it as a Category A Bioterrorism Agent
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioterrorism>.^[5]
<#cite_note-CategoryBioterrorism-5> It is also listed as a biological
agent for export control by the Australia Group
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia_Group>.^[6]
<#cite_note-AustraliaGroup-6>

The virus can be transmitted by exposure to one species of fruit bats or
it can be transmitted between people via body fluids through unprotected
copulation and broken skin. The disease can cause bleeding
(haemorrhage), fever and other symptoms much like Ebola. Funeral rituals
are a particular risk. Actual treatment of the virus after infection is
not possible but early, professional treatment of symptoms like
dehydration considerably increase survival chances.^[7] <#cite_note-7>

In 2009, expanded clinical trials
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clinical_trial> of an Ebola
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ebola> and Marburg vaccine
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaccine> began in Kampala
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kampala>, Uganda
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uganda>.^[8] <#cite_note-8> ^[9]
<#cite_note-9>

    Discovery[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marburg_virus&action=edit&section=1>]

Marburg virus was first described in 1967.^[10]
<#cite_note-Siegert1967-10> It was noticed during small outbreaks in the
German cities Marburg <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marburg> and
Frankfurt <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankfurt> and the Yugoslav
capital Belgrade <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgrade> in the 1960s.
German workers were exposed to tissues of infected grivet monkeys
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grivet> (/Chlorocebus aethiops/) at the
city's former main industrial plant, the Behringwerke, then part of
Hoechst <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hoechst_AG>, and today of CSL
Behring <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CSL_Behring>. During these
outbreaks, 31 people became infected and seven of them died. MARV is a
Select Agent <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Select_Agent>.^[11]
<#cite_note-SelectAgents-11>


      Nomenclature[edit
      <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marburg_virus&action=edit&section=2>]

The virus is one of two members of the species
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Committee_on_Taxonomy_of_Viruses>
/Marburg marburgvirus
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marburg_marburgvirus>/, which is included
in the genus
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Committee_on_Taxonomy_of_Viruses>
/Marburgvirus <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marburgvirus>/, family
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Committee_on_Taxonomy_of_Viruses>
/Filoviridae <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filoviridae>/, order
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Committee_on_Taxonomy_of_Viruses>
/Mononegavirales <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mononegavirales>/. The
name Marburg virus is derived from Marburg
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marburg> (the city in Hesse
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hesse>, Germany, where the virus was
first discovered) and the taxonomic
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)> suffix
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suffix> /virus/.^[1] <#cite_note-KuhnArch-1>

According to the rules for taxon naming established by the International
Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses (ICTV)
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Committee_on_Taxonomy_of_Viruses>,
the name Marburg virus is always to be capitalized
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalization>, but is never italicized
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italic_type>, and may be abbreviated
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbreviation> (with MARV being the
official abbreviation).^[/citation needed
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed>/]

Marburg virus was first introduced under this name in 1967.^[10]
<#cite_note-Siegert1967-10> In 2005, the virus name was changed to Lake
Victoria marburgvirus, which unfortunately was the same spelling as its
species /Lake Victoria marburgvirus/.^[12] <#cite_note-Feldmann2005-12>
^[13] <#cite_note-13> However, most scientific articles continued to
refer to Marburg virus. Consequently, in 2010, the name Marburg virus
was reinstated and the species name changed.^[1] <#cite_note-KuhnArch-1>


    Human disease[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marburg_virus&action=edit&section=3>]

Main article: Marburg virus disease
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marburg_virus_disease>

MARV is one of two Marburg viruses that causes Marburg virus disease
(MVD) <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marburg_virus_disease> in humans
(in the literature also often referred to as Marburg hemorrhagic fever,
MHF). The other one is Ravn virus
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ravn_virus> (RAVV). Both viruses fulfill
the criteria for being a member of the species Marburg marburgvirus
because their genomes <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome> diverge
from the prototype Marburg marburgvirus or the Marburg virus variant
Musoke (MARV/Mus) by <10% at the nucleotide
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleotide> level.^[1]
<#cite_note-KuhnArch-1>

    Virology[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marburg_virus&action=edit&section=5>]


      Genome[edit
      <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marburg_virus&action=edit&section=6>]

Like all mononegaviruses
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mononegavirales>, marburgvirions contain
non-infectious, linear nonsegmented, single-stranded RNA
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA> genomes
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome> of negative polarity that possess
inverse-complementary 3' and 5' termini, do not possess a 5' cap
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5%27_cap>, are not polyadenylated
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyadenylation>, and are not covalently
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond> linked to a protein
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein>.^[46]
<#cite_note-Fauquet2005-46> Marburgvirus genomes are approximately 19 kb
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_pair> long and contain seven genes
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene> in the order 3'-UTR
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_prime_untranslated_region>-/NP/-/VP35/-/VP40/-/GP/-/VP30/-/VP24/-/L/-5'-UTR
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_prime_untranslated_region>.^[47]
<#cite_note-Kiley1982-47>


      Structure[edit
      <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marburg_virus&action=edit&section=7>]

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Marburg_em1986.png>

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Marburg_em1986.png>
CryoEM reconstruction of a section of the Marburg virus nucleocapsid.
EMDB <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EMDB> entry EMD-1986
<https://www.ebi.ac.uk/pdbe/entry/pdb/EMD-1986>^[48] <#cite_note-48>

Like all filoviruses <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filoviridae>,
marburgvirions are filamentous particles that may appear in the shape of
a shepherd's crook or in the shape of a "U" or a "6", and they may be
coiled, toroid, or branched.^[47] <#cite_note-Kiley1982-47>
Marburgvirions are generally 80 nm in width
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Width>, but vary somewhat in length. In
general, the median particle length of marburgviruses ranges from 795 to
828 nm (in contrast to ebolavirions
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ebolavirus>, whose median particle length
was measured to be 974–1,086 nm ), but particles as long as 14,000 nm
have been detected in tissue culture.^[49] <#cite_note-Geisbert1995-49>
Marburgvirions consist of seven structural proteins. At the center is
the helical <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helix> ribonucleocapsid
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribonucleoprotein>, which consists of the
genomic RNA wrapped around a polymer
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymer> of nucleoproteins
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleoprotein> (NP). Associated with the
ribonucleoprotein is the RNA-dependent RNA polymerase
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA-dependent_RNA_polymerase> (L) with
the polymerase cofactor (VP35) and a transcription activator (VP30). The
ribonucleoprotein is embedded in a matrix, formed by the major (VP40)
and minor (VP24) matrix proteins. These particles are surrounded by a
lipid membrane <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid_bilayer> derived
from the host cell membrane. The membrane anchors a glycoprotein (GP_1,2
) that projects 7 to 10 nm spikes away from its surface. While nearly
identical to ebolavirions in structure, marburgvirions are antigenically
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antigen> distinct.^[/citation needed
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed>/]


      Entry[edit
      <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marburg_virus&action=edit&section=8>]

Niemann–Pick C1 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NPC1> (NPC1) cholesterol
transporter protein appears to be essential for infection with both
Ebola <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ebola_virus> and Marburg virus. Two
independent studies reported in the same issue of /Nature
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature_(journal)>/ showed that Ebola
virus <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ebola_virus> cell entry and
replication requires NPC1.^[50] <#cite_note-pmid21866103-50> ^[51]
<#cite_note-pmid21866101-51> When cells from patients lacking NPC1 were
exposed to Ebola virus in the laboratory, the cells survived and
appeared immune to the virus <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus>,
further indicating that Ebola relies on NPC1 to enter cells. This might
imply that genetic mutations in the NPC1 gene in humans could make some
people resistant to one of the deadliest known viruses affecting humans.
The same studies described similar results with Marburg virus, showing
that it also needs NPC1 to enter cells.^[50]
<#cite_note-pmid21866103-50> ^[51] <#cite_note-pmid21866101-51>
Furthermore, NPC1 was shown to be critical to filovirus
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filovirus> entry because it mediates
infection by binding directly to the viral envelope
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viral_envelope> glycoprotein^[51]
<#cite_note-pmid21866101-51> and that the second lysosomal domain of
NPC1 mediates this binding.^[52] <#cite_note-pmid22395071-52>

In one of the original studies, a small molecule
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_molecule> was shown to inhibit
Ebola virus infection by preventing the virus glycoprotein from binding
to NPC1.^[51] <#cite_note-pmid21866101-51> ^[53]
<#cite_note-pmid21959282-53> In the other study, mice that were
heterozygous for NPC1 were shown to be protected from lethal challenge
with mouse-adapted Ebola virus.^[50] <#cite_note-pmid21866103-50>


      Replication[edit
      <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marburg_virus&action=edit&section=9>]

The marburg virus life cycle
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_life_cycle> begins with virion
attachment to specific cell-surface receptors
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Receptor_(biochemistry)>, followed by
fusion <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid_bilayer_fusion> of the
virion envelope with cellular membranes and the concomitant release of
the virus nucleocapsid <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleocapsid> into
the cytosol <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytosol>. The virus RdRp
partially uncoats the nucleocapsid and transcribes
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)> the genes
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genes> into positive-stranded mRNAs
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MRNA>, which are then translated
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(biology)> into structural
and nonstructural proteins <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proteins>.
Marburgvirus L binds to a single promoter
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Promoter_(biology)> located at the 3' end
of the genome. Transcription either terminates after a gene or continues
to the next gene downstream. This means that genes close to the 3' end
of the genome are transcribed in the greatest abundance, whereas those
toward the 5' end are least likely to be transcribed. The gene order is
therefore a simple but effective form of transcriptional regulation. The
most abundant protein produced is the nucleoprotein
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleoprotein>, whose concentration
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concentration> in the cell determines
when L switches from gene transcription to genome replication.
Replication results in full-length, positive-stranded antigenomes that
are in turn transcribed into negative-stranded virus progeny genome
copies. Newly synthesized structural proteins and genomes self-assemble
and accumulate near the inside of the cell membrane
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_membrane>. Virions bud
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budding> off from the cell, gaining their
envelopes from the cellular membrane they bud from. The mature progeny
particles then infect other cells to repeat the cycle.^[12]
<#cite_note-Feldmann2005-12>


    Ecology[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marburg_virus&action=edit&section=10>]

In 2009, the successful isolation of infectious MARV was reported from
caught healthy Egyptian fruit bats (/Rousettus aegyptiacus/)
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rousettus_aegyptiacus>.^[39]
<#cite_note-Towner2009-39> This isolation, together with the isolation
of infectious RAVV <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RAVV>,^[39]
<#cite_note-Towner2009-39> strongly suggests that Old World
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_World> fruit bats
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bat> are involved in the natural
maintenance of marburgviruses. Further studies are necessary to
establish whether Egyptian rousettes are the actual hosts of MARV and
RAVV or whether they get infected via contact with another animal and
therefore serve only as intermediate hosts. Recently the first
experimental infection study of /Rousettus aegyptiacus/ with MARV
provided further insight into the possible involvement of these bats in
MARV ecology.^[54] <#cite_note-54> Experimentally infected bats
developed relatively low viremia lasting at least 5 days, but remained
healthy and didn't develop any notable gross pathology. The virus also
replicated to high titers in major organs (liver and spleen), and organs
that might possibly be involved in virus transmission (lung, intestine,
reproductive organ, salivary gland, kidney, bladder and mammary gland).
The relatively long period of viremia noted in this experiment could
possibly also facilitate mechanical transmission by blood sucking
arthropods or infection of susceptible vertebrate hosts by direct
contact with infected blood.^[/citation needed
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed>/]


    Evolution[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marburg_virus&action=edit&section=11>]

The viral strains fall into two clades — Ravn virus and Marburg
virus.^[55] <#cite_note-Zehender2016-55> The Marburg strains can be
divided into two — A and B. The A strains were isolated from Uganda
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uganda> (five from 1967), Kenya
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenya> (1980) and Angola
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angola> (2004–2005) while the B strains
were from the Democratic Republic of the Congo
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo>
epidemic (1999–2000) and a group of Ugandan isolates isolated in
2007-2009. The mean evolutionary rate of the whole genome was 3.3 ×
10^−4 substitutions/site/year (credibility interval 2.0–4.8).The Marburg
strains had a mean root time of the most recent common ancestor of 177.9
years ago (95% highest posterior density 87–284) suggesting an origin in
the mid 1800s. In contrast the Ravn strains origin dated back to a mean
33.8 years ago (early 1980s). The most probable location of the Marburg
virus ancestor was Uganda whereas that of the RAVV ancestor was
Kenya.^[/citation needed
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed>/]


    Biological weapon[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marburg_virus&action=edit&section=12>]

The Soviet Union <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union> had an
extensive offensive and defensive biological weapons
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_warfare> program that included
MARV.^[56] <#cite_note-Alibek1999-56> At least three Soviet research
institutes had MARV research programs during the Cold War
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War>: the Virology Center of the
Scientific-Research Institute for Microbiology in Zagorsk (today Sergiev
Posad <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergiev_Posad>), the
Scientific-Production Association "Vektor" (today the State Research
Center of Virology and Biotechnology "Vektor"
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_Research_Center_of_Virology_and_Biotechnology_VECTOR>)
in Koltsovo
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koltsovo,_Novosibirsk_Oblast>, and the
Irkutsk Scientific-Research Anti-Plague Institute of Siberia and the Far
East in Irkutsk <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irkutsk>. As most
performed research was highly classified
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classified_information>, it remains
unclear how successful the MARV program was. However, Soviet defector
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defection> Ken Alibek
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ken_Alibek> claimed that a weapon filled
with MARV was tested at the Stepnogorsk Scientific Experimental and
Production Base
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stepnagorsk_Scientific_and_Technical_Institute_for_Microbiology>
in Stepnogorsk <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stepnogorsk>, Kazakh
Soviet Socialist Republic
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakh_Soviet_Socialist_Republic> (today
Kazakhstan <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan>),^[56]
<#cite_note-Alibek1999-56> suggesting that the development of a MARV
biological weapon had reached advanced stages. Independent confirmation
for this claim is lacking. At least one laboratory accident with MARV,
resulting in the death of Koltsovo researcher Nikolai Ustinov, occurred
during the Cold War in the Soviet Union and was first described in
detail by Alibek.^[56] <#cite_note-Alibek1999-56>
